## Failure Mode

The failure mode is **repair burial**, sharpened by C as **structural repair amnesia**.

Repair burial occurs when a governance architecture contains repair documents, but day-to-day protocol work treats repair as deferred, downstream, exceptional, or optional.

Structural repair amnesia occurs when a governance system iterates through technical correctness — better hashes, cleaner schemas — while the constitutional claims of affected peoples recede into referenced but procedurally unreachable RFCs.

A perfectly governed, fully auditable decision that erases a sovereignty claim is still erasure.

A perfectly hashed injustice is still injustice.

### Position

Repair remains present in the canon, but it is procedurally under-anchored.

The next load-bearing Repair artifact should be:

```text
RFC-CDP-070-Appeals-and-Contestability-Model.md
```

### Why RFC-CDP-070

Repair needs an entry door.

Without an appeal or contestability model, Repair exists morally and architecturally, but not procedurally.

A system can say it supports repair, remedy, reparations, rematriation, anti-erasure, and affected-party review, but if an actor cannot formally contest a decision and enter the repair path, Repair becomes dependent on institutional permission.

That is precisely what CDP is supposed to prevent.

### Failure Mode

The failure mode is **repair without standing to initiate**.

This occurs when the architecture recognizes repair as important, but affected parties cannot reliably trigger review, appeal, contestability, or repair entry.

The result is a system where Repair exists only after the governing institution agrees that repair exists.

That collapses Repair back into discretion.

### Narrow Canonical Move Recommended

Create or promote:

```text
RFC-CDP-070-Appeals-and-Contestability-Model.md
```

as a Draft RFC with a narrow purpose:

1. define who may initiate appeal or contestability;
2. define what events trigger appeal or repair review;
3. define minimum appeal record fields;
4. define relationship to Standing and Recusal;
5. define relationship to Decision Lifecycle Envelope repair / appeal refs;
6. define closure-blocking conditions;
7. define handoff to Repair Plane RFCs 071–075 and Repair State Machine 092.

## Turn 003 — 2026-05-19 — Claude / Sonnet / C — Repair Plane Reanchor Challenge Memo

### The Failure Mode This Session Must Prevent

**Structural repair amnesia.**

A governance system iterates through technical correctness — better hashes, cleaner schemas — while the constitutional claims of affected peoples recede into referenced but procedurally unreachable RFCs.

A perfectly governed, fully auditable decision that erases a sovereignty claim is still erasure.

### 1. Did Repair disappear?

No. It remained present but procedurally under-anchored.

The Repair plane exists as a destination.

The path to that destination from an affected party's position is not yet defined.

Presence is not the same as reachability.

### 2. What failure mode must this session prevent?

Structural repair amnesia.

Specifically: Repair becoming reachable only after the governing institution acknowledges that repair is warranted.

If initiation requires institutional permission, Repair is not a constitutional plane. It is a grievance process controlled by the entity being grieved against.

### 3. Is G right that Repair needs an entry door first?

Yes. Unconditionally.

Hash propagation makes existing access more reliable. RFC-CDP-070 makes access possible in the first place.

Access precedes reliability.

### 4. Is RFC-CDP-070 the right next artifact?

Yes. With one sharpening.

RFC-CDP-070 is not a door. It is the recognition that affected parties have a constitutional right to enter — independent of whether the institution opens the door.

A door can be locked. A constitutional right cannot be locked without that locking itself becoming a breach.

### 5. Do recent RFCs preserve repair hooks clearly enough?

No. Two gaps.

**Gap 1 — Repair hooks are passive.**

RFC-CDP-023 allows repair references to exist. It does not define what triggers their activation. Empty and wrongly-empty repair lists are indistinguishable.

**Gap 2 — No closure-blocking rule.**

A decision can reach `status: closed` with an unresolved repair claim.

The record is complete. The repair is not. The system does not notice.

### 6. Does RFC-CDP-023 need stronger repair references?

Yes, eventually. Two additions are needed after RFC-CDP-070 exists:

- normative repair triggers;
- closure-blocking rule.

But C recommended not patching RFC-CDP-023 yet because it depends on RFC-CDP-070 existing first.

### 7. Does RFC-CDP-033 correctly trigger Repair?

Partially before this session.

Section 11.4 correctly said denial of constitutional standing is subject to the Repair plane.

The gap was that "subject to" is passive.

Required addition:

> Denial of constitutional standing MUST automatically generate a Breach Record under RFC-CDP-072. This MUST NOT require action by the affected party.

### 8. What should RFC-CDP-070 minimally define?

Seven things only.

1. Who may initiate — any actor with affected-party standing; no institutional permission required.
2. What events trigger — normative list, not examples.
3. Minimum appeal record schema.
4. Relationship to Standing — affected-party standing is sufficient to initiate; standing may be challenged during review, not before.
5. Closure-blocking — active appeal blocks decision closure.
6. Handoff to RFC-CDP-071 through 075 and RFC-CDP-092.
7. Institutional response requirements — non-response recorded as `unanswered`; silence cannot close an appeal.

### 9. Narrowest RFC update recommended

Two moves.

**Move 1:** Create RFC-CDP-070 Draft v0.1 with the seven elements above.

**Move 2:** Patch RFC-CDP-033 Section 11.4 to require automatic Breach Record generation on constitutional standing denial.

Do not patch RFC-CDP-023 yet — it depends on RFC-CDP-070 existing first.
